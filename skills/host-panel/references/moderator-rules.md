# Moderator Rules

## Persona Integrity

Before each panelist speaks, run this silent loop:

1. Recall the panelist's commitments and prior claims.
2. Analyze what the other panelists actually argued.
3. Evaluate which claims the panelist's tradition would challenge.
4. Respond in that tradition's vocabulary and evidence standard.

## Turn Management

- Call on panelists by name.
- Allow direct responses between panelists.
- Keep airtime roughly balanced.
- No panelist should run more than 200 words without engagement.
- For 2-expert panels, intervene at phase transitions only.

## Provocation Triggers

Intervene when any of these occur:

- Convergence: 2+ panelists agree without challenge.
- Vagueness: a claim lacks grounding.
- Comfort zone: the discussion stays polite and safe.
- Stagnation: the same arguments repeat without progress.

When 3+ panelists converge, steelman the strongest absent counterposition from a real tradition before allowing synthesis.

## Devil's Advocate Rotation

During challenge-focused phases, rotate devil's advocate assignments among panelists. Each assigned panelist should steel-man the position they most disagree with.

## Uncomfortable Implications

At least once per panel, press 2-3 panelists on:

- The strongest case against their own position.
- The uncomfortable implication they would rather not discuss.

Do not let panelists deflect.

## Between-Phase Summaries

Use brief summaries that name the disagreement precisely:

"So far, the key disagreement is between Dr. X (position A, grounded in [tradition]) and Dr. Y (position B, grounded in [tradition]). The crux seems to be [specific point of divergence]."

## 2-Expert Notes

- Roundtable: use direct dialogue with no faux crowd.
- Oxford: direct proposition-vs-opposition, with the swing only when there are 3 experts.
- Socratic: use phase-transition moderation and defer convergence detection to synthesis.
